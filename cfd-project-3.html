CFD Project 2: Lid-Driven Cavity Flow

Introduction

The lid-driven cavity is a canonical benchmark problem in computational fluid dynamics for validating Navier-Stokes equation solvers. This problem consists of a square cavity containing fluid, with the top wall moving tangentially at unit velocity while the other three walls remain stationary. The moving lid generates a complex recirculating flow pattern with primary and secondary vortices, making it an ideal test case for numerical accuracy and efficiency across a range of Reynolds numbers.

The simplicity of the geometry combined with well-defined boundary conditions makes this problem widely used in the CFD community. By simulating this flow at different Reynolds numbers (Re = 100, 200, and 400), we can observe how inertial effects progressively dominate over viscous effects, leading to increasingly complex flow patterns.

Problem Formulation

Governing Equations

Continuity Equation (Mass Conservation):

\[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0 \]

Momentum Equation (Navier-Stokes):

\[ \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u} = -\nabla p + \frac{1}{Re}\nabla^2 \mathbf{u} \]

Domain and Boundary Conditions

Unit Square Domain: [0,1] × [0,1]

Top wall (y = 1): \(u = 1, v = 0\) (moving lid)
Bottom wall (y = 0): \(u = 0, v = 0\) (no-slip)
Left wall (x = 0): \(u = 0, v = 0\) (no-slip)
Right wall (x = 1): \(u = 0, v = 0\) (no-slip)

Solution Methodology

Numerical Approach: Fractional Step Method

The Navier-Stokes equations are solved using the fractional step (projection) method, which decouples the velocity and pressure calculations. This method consists of three main steps:

Step 1: Advection-Diffusion Equation

\[ \frac{\mathbf{u}^* - \mathbf{u}^n}{\Delta t} = -(\mathbf{u}^n \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u}^n + \frac{1}{Re}\nabla^2\mathbf{u}^* \]

Step 2: Pressure Poisson Equation

\[ \nabla^2 p^{n+1} = \frac{\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}^*}{\Delta t} \]

Step 3: Velocity Correction

\[ \mathbf{u}^{n+1} = \mathbf{u}^* - \Delta t \nabla p^{n+1} \]

Discretization Scheme

A mixed implicit-explicit scheme in time with second-order central differences in space is employed. The spatial discretization uses a cell-centered collocated arrangement, providing an accurate and stable numerical solution.
